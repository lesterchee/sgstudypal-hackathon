# Probate vs. Divorce: Asset Treatment

While both Probate (specifically concerning Wills and Intestacy) and Divorce deal with the distribution of assets, the legal principles guiding these two domains are fundamentally different. The intention of the deceased (or the strict rules of intestacy) governs Probate, while fairness and contribution to the marriage govern Divorce.

Below are the key differences in how specific types of assets are treated.

## 1. Joint Tenancy Properties

### In Probate (Wills & Intestacy)
- **Rule of Survivorship:** Properties held in Joint Tenancy completely **bypass** the Will and the Intestate Succession Act.
- **Outcome:** Upon the death of one joint tenant, the property automatically vests in the surviving joint tenant(s). The deceased cannot leave their "share" to someone else in a Will, because they do not have a distinct, severable share at the moment of death. 

### In Divorce
- **Matrimonial Pool:** The property is fully included in the matrimonial asset pool, regardless of whether it's held in Joint Tenancy or Tenancy-in-Common.
- **Outcome:** The Family Justice Courts will divide the property based on the principles of Section 112 of the Women's Charter (e.g., direct and indirect contributions), irrespective of the legal holding structure. The "Survivorship" rule is irrelevant here.

## 2. CPF Funds (Central Provident Fund)

### In Probate (Wills & Intestacy)
- **Nominations Prevail:** CPF funds **cannot** be distributed via a Will. If a deceased made a valid CPF nomination, the funds go directly to the nominees.
- **Intestacy Fallback:** Only if there is *no* valid nomination will the CPF funds be transferred to the Public Trustee for distribution according to the Intestate Succession Act.

### In Divorce
- **Matrimonial Asset:** CPF balances accumulated during the marriage (minus pre-marriage balances if not commingled or used for the matrimonial home) are fully considered as matrimonial assets subject to division.
- **Outcome:** The court can order the transfer of a portion of one spouse's CPF funds to the other spouse's CPF account, or offset the value against other assets like the matrimonial home.

## 3. Nominated Life Insurance Policies (Section 73 CLPA vs. Section 49L Insurance Act)

### In Probate (Wills & Intestacy)
- **Trust Policies:** If a policy was created under Section 73 of the Conveyancing and Law of Property Act (CLPA) or Section 49L of the Insurance Act (with spouse/children as nominees), a statutory trust is created.
- **Outcome:** The policy proceeds do not form part of the deceased's estate. They bypass the Will and the Intestate Succession Act and go directly to the nominees.

### In Divorce
- **Cash Value:** The cash surrender value of life insurance policies (acquired during the marriage or where premiums were paid during the marriage) is typically included in the matrimonial asset pool.
- **Outcome:** The court may order a policy to be surrendered, transferred, or its value offset against other assets. The existence of a nomination does not automatically exclude it from the pool if the premiums were paid using matrimonial funds.

## Summary

The fundamental difference lies in **Ownership vs. Equitable Division**:
- **Probate law** is concerned with strict legal ownership at the time of death and the passing of that exact legal title (subject to specific carve-outs like survivorship and CPF rules).
- **Divorce law** under the Women's Charter uses "equitable division," empowering the court to look past legal title and holding structures to divide assets fairly based on the partnership of the marriage.
